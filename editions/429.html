Your Daily Artificial Intelligence Gazette

AI Daily Briefing — June 25, 2026

The AI talent war is heating up, chip geopolitics are reshaping global supply chains, and developers are getting meaningful quality-of-life upgrades across the Claude toolchain. Meanwhile, new data is quietly dismantling one of AI's most persistent narratives: that it would hollow out engineering employment.

Industry Moves

Google's brain drain continues. AI researchers Jonas Adler and Alexander Pritzel are departing Google for Anthropic, continuing a pattern that includes high-profile exits like Noam Shazeer and John Jumper. The steady flow of talent away from DeepMind and Google Research toward upstart rivals suggests compensation, autonomy, and research culture remain differentiators that brand prestige alone can't overcome.

Vishal Sikka bets on agentic IT services. The former Infosys CEO has launched a new startup backed by Mayfield and Aramco Ventures, assembling veterans from SAP, Infosys, and his previous AI venture VianAI. The play appears to be displacing traditional IT services with AI-native delivery — a direct challenge to the legacy model Sikka helped build.

Cerebras stumbles on its public debut. Shares dropped sharply after the AI chipmaker's first earnings report as a public company, with investors spooked by a narrower-than-expected gross margin forecast in its core business. CEO Andrew Feldman insists the market misread the guidance — but in a climate of high AI hardware expectations, the communication gap was costly.

Chips & Geopolitics

Europe pushes back on Washington's chip export controls. As ASML CEO Christophe Fouquet has noted, China's current access is limited to older deep ultraviolet (DUV) lithography tools from roughly a decade ago — but European chipmakers and governments are increasingly resistant to U.S. pressure to tighten restrictions further. The tension pits transatlantic alliance politics against Europe's economic interests in maintaining market access and sovereign industrial policy.

Memory chip scarcity is minting a U.S. winner. One American memory chipmaker saw revenue quadruple to $41.45 billion year-over-year, with profit surging from $1.88 billion to $28.2 billion — extraordinary numbers that reflect just how tight the AI-driven memory supply chain has become. The crunch is a reminder that the hardware bottleneck in the AI buildout isn't just about GPUs.

Workforce & Economics

Engineers are actually gaining ground, not losing it. New data from SignalFire shows that engineers are making up a larger share of new hires even as AI dominates layoff headlines in other functions. The finding complicates the displacement narrative and aligns with the view that AI is currently amplifying engineering output rather than replacing the humans doing it.

The token budget reckoning is here. Companies are scrambling to implement AI usage controls after discovering that employees are burning through enterprise AI allocations on trivial tasks. The "tokenmaxxing" phase — where unlimited access led to profligate prompting — is giving way to rationing policies, usage dashboards, and per-team budget caps.

Policy & Governance

Claude caught in a Capitol Hill AI-authorship controversy. Rep. Anna Paulina Luna (R-FL) is pushing back on reports that her staff used Anthropic's Claude to write an amendment summary for a major defense funding bill, insisting AI was only used for "spellcheck" and that "NO Legislation is ever written by AI." The episode highlights the murky line between AI assistance and AI authorship — and the political sensitivity around that distinction in legislative contexts.

Claude Code Developer Corner

v2.1.191 drops with /rewind, agent fixes, and scroll sanity. The headline addition in Claude Code v2.1.191 is /rewind — a long-requested feature that lets you resume a conversation from before a /clear was run. This is a meaningful workflow change: previously, clearing a session was irreversible mid-work, forcing users to reconstruct context manually. Now you can step back without losing your thread.

Two important bug fixes ship alongside: background agents no longer resurrect after being stopped from the tasks panel (a persistent reliability issue that could leave ghost agents consuming resources), and the scroll position no longer jumps to the bottom when reading earlier output during a streaming response. The latter is a quality-of-life fix that makes reviewing long outputs while a response is still generating significantly less disorienting.

Two additional polish fixes: /voice now surfaces a proper policy-restriction message when disabled by an org (rather than a generic "not available" error), and /login URLs no longer get truncated in Windows Terminal — a small but blocking issue for Windows-based developers onboarding to Claude Code.

Python and TypeScript SDKs add system.message streaming event support. Both anthropic-sdk-python v0.112.0 and anthropic-sdk-typescript v0.106.0 ship the same core feature: support for system.message streaming events on the client. This enables developers building streaming pipelines to handle system-level messages in real time rather than waiting for a completed response — useful for agentic workflows where you need to react to model state changes as they happen. Both SDKs appear to be keeping tight feature parity, which is worth noting if you're maintaining implementations in both languages.

Worth Watching

OpenAI Codex is wearing out SSDs. A report from The Register details how OpenAI's Codex is generating excessive and unnecessary SSD write operations in its sandboxed environments, with the cumulative cost potentially running into the millions. It's a concrete example of the hidden infrastructure costs that emerge when AI tooling isn't optimized at the system level — and a useful cautionary tale for teams running self-hosted coding agents at scale.
